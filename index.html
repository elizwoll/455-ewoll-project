Sign Painting 101

"Sign Painting 101" is a captivating introduction to the timeless art of hand-painted signs, offering beginners and enthusiasts a look into the foundations of this traditional craft. The video walks viewers through the basic tools, materials, and techniques needed to get started—covering everything from brush handling and paint consistency to layout tips and lettering styles.

As the instructor demonstrates each step, the video emphasizes the value of patience, precision, and practice. You'll learn how to prepare surfaces, sketch letterforms, and apply paint with steady strokes to create clean, consistent lines. Whether you're aiming to make your first shop sign or simply curious about the process, this video makes the craft approachable and inspiring.

Beyond technique, "Sign Painting 101" also touches on the history and culture behind the craft—highlighting how this analog art form continues to thrive in a digital age. With its blend of practical instruction and artistic insight, the video serves as both a how-to guide and a tribute to the enduring beauty of hand lettering. It’s perfect for artists, makers, and anyone drawn to creative, hands-on expression.

Contemporary Sign Painting

"Sign Painting" offers an insightful exploration into the world of sign making, highlighting the artistry and craftsmanship involved in creating iconic signage. The video delves into the intricate processes of hand-painting and gilding, showcasing the meticulous attention to detail that transforms a simple sign into a work of art. Viewers are introduced to the tools and techniques used by skilled sign makers, providing a behind-the-scenes look at this often-overlooked craft.​

Through interviews with experienced sign makers, the video emphasizes the significance of signage in shaping the identity of a community. It highlights how signs not only serve functional purposes but also contribute to the aesthetic and cultural fabric of a place. The video also touches upon the resurgence of interest in traditional sign-making methods, as people seek authenticity and uniqueness in an increasingly digital world.​

Overall, "Sign of the Times" is a celebration of the enduring appeal of handcrafted signage. It serves as a reminder of the importance of preserving traditional crafts and the stories they tell about our communities and histories. The video is a must-watch for anyone interested in design, craftsmanship, or the cultural significance of signage.​

Neon Signmaking

"How to Make Neon Signs" takes viewers behind the glow to reveal the fascinating process of crafting custom neon signage. From bending glass tubes over an open flame to filling them with noble gases, this video offers a rare, up-close look at a craft that blends science, art, and serious hands-on skill. It's a deep dive into a luminous tradition that continues to shine bright in the age of LEDs.

The video walks through each stage of the process, starting with design and layout, then moving into tube bending, electrode attachment, gas filling, and final wiring. As the artist explains each step, you'll gain insight into the tools of the trade and the precision required to create those smooth curves and glowing lines. It's a process that requires a steady hand, a practiced eye, and a lot of heat—literally.

More than just a tutorial, "How to Make Neon Signs" celebrates the legacy and resilience of neon artistry. It honors the makers who keep the flame alive and reminds us why real neon—buzzing, flickering, and handmade—still holds such a unique place in signage and visual culture. Whether you're a designer, DIY enthusiast, or neon lover, this video is pure eye candy with substance behind the shine.

Museum of Neon Art

"The Museum of Neon Art in Los Angeles" offers an illuminating journey into the vibrant world of neon signage and light-based art. Located in Glendale, California, this unique museum showcases a rich collection of historic neon signs, kinetic sculptures, and electric artworks that illuminate the cultural tapestry of Los Angeles.

The video delves into the museum's diverse exhibits, including pieces that narrate the stories of immigrants and entrepreneurs who utilized neon to attract customers, reflecting the city's dynamic history. Highlights include the iconic Brown Derby sign and the animated "Rugs" sign from Pasadena Rug Mart, each telling tales of a bygone era .

Beyond its exhibits, the museum plays a pivotal role in preserving this endangered craft through hands-on classes in neon bending and advocacy for the restoration of historic signage. It also offers guided tours, such as the Neon Night Walks and Neon Cruises, allowing visitors to experience the glowing legacy of Los Angeles firsthand .

Whether you're an art enthusiast, a history buff, or someone captivated by the glow of neon, this video provides a comprehensive look at the Museum of Neon Art and its mission to keep the glow alive for future generations.

History of Sign Making

The history of sign making in Santa Cruz and the greater Bay Area is a story deeply intertwined with the region’s cultural identity, artistic movements, and entrepreneurial spirit. From the quirky surf shops of the Santa Cruz boardwalk to the bustling neon glow of historic Oakland theaters, signage in this part of California has long served as both a functional necessity and a creative expression of place. Across decades, sign makers have helped shape the visual language of coastal towns and urban centers alike, turning everyday advertisements into works of art.

In the late 19th and early 20th centuries, as towns like Santa Cruz and San Jose grew into commercial and transportation hubs, the need for signage blossomed. Local businesses—from saloons and soda fountains to hotels and haberdasheries—commissioned hand-painted wooden signs to hang above their doors or in storefront windows. These early signs were often created by itinerant sign painters, known for their precise brushwork and ability to design letters by eye. Their tools were simple—mahl sticks, quills, and oil-based paints—but the results were highly personal and often beautifully detailed.

20's and 30's

As the automobile age took off in the 1920s and ’30s, signage in the Bay Area evolved along with the landscape. Highway 1 and Route 17 brought tourists through Santa Cruz and up into San Francisco, prompting businesses to invest in bolder, more visible signage. Neon signage became a defining visual element of the era. In San Francisco and Oakland, massive neon marquees lit up theater districts, while Santa Cruz saw an increase in bright, glowing signs advertising seafood restaurants, motels, and boardwalk amusements. The buzz of neon became a beacon for nightlife, entertainment, and local commerce throughout the region.

Santa Cruz

Santa Cruz, with its strong surf and skate culture, developed a distinctive style of signage—casual, colorful, and hand-drawn. The beach-town vibe of Santa Cruz fostered a design language that was playful and bold, often influenced by comic art and DIY punk zine aesthetics.

Local surf shops, cafes, and tattoo parlors used hand-painted signs and funky typography to set themselves apart, contributing to the eclectic character the town is known for today. Many of these businesses relied on local sign painters, some of whom developed reputations as artists in their own right.

Greater Bay Area

Throughout the Bay Area, and especially in San Francisco’s Mission District and Berkeley’s Telegraph Avenue, hand lettering maintained its relevance well into the late 20th century. Sign painters played a crucial role in shaping the visual personality of the region’s independent bookstores, record shops, taquerias, and coffee houses. These hand-crafted signs told stories—not just about what was being sold, but about the communities they represented. Even as vinyl and digital printing became more widespread in the 1980s and '90s, there remained a strong grassroots movement to preserve traditional craftsmanship and analog design.

Today

In recent years, that movement has only grown stronger. Across the Bay Area, a new generation of sign makers and lettering artists has emerged, blending old-school techniques with contemporary aesthetics. In Santa Cruz, businesses like Sign of the Times have become local institutions—bringing hand-painted signage back into the mainstream by celebrating its authenticity and charm. These studios not only produce signs for modern clients but also help preserve and restore historic signage, bridging the gap between eras.

Meanwhile, organizations such as the Letterform Archive in San Francisco and the The Museum of Neon Art in nearby Los Angeles have helped elevate sign making to the realm of serious design and visual history. Local workshops, lettering classes, and public art initiatives have brought renewed appreciation for the hand-drawn letter and the artisan behind it. Even in the tech-driven heart of Silicon Valley, there's growing recognition that something handcrafted—especially in signage—carries emotional and cultural weight that digital design often lacks. Today, the signs of Santa Cruz and the Bay Area are more than just directions or advertisements—they’re icons of place, memory, and identity. Whether it’s a faded ghost sign on a Mission Street wall, a retro neon diner light flickering in Oakland, or a freshly painted window in downtown Santa Cruz, each sign tells a story. These pieces of visual history remind us that even in a fast-changing world, there’s beauty in slowing down, picking up a brush, and making something that lasts.
